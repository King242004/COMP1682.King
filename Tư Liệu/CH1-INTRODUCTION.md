## 1.1 General Overview

Managing a diet related chronic condition is a daily activity conducted almost entirely outside clinical supervision. A person diagnosed with type 2 diabetes, hypertension or gout receives dietary guidance at a consultation, then applies that guidance unaided across the hundreds of food decisions that occur before the next appointment. The quality of those decisions materially affects clinical outcomes, yet the individual making them has no ready means of checking a specific meal against their specific condition at the moment of eating.

Mobile applications are the established response to this gap, and a substantial market of nutrition tracking products exists. These products are, however, constructed principally around measurement. They record what has been consumed and compute totals against a target. The interpretive question that matters most to a person with a medical condition, namely whether a particular dish is suitable for them, is left unanswered.

This project develops and evaluates MealMate, a cross platform mobile application in which a user's declared chronic condition operates as an active constraint on dietary guidance rather than as a passive profile field. The application combines photograph based recognition of Vietnamese dishes with condition aware advice generated by a large language model and constrained by an independent verification layer.

## 1.2 Problem Statement

Vietnamese adults managing diet related chronic conditions struggle to determine whether a specific everyday meal is suitable for their individual condition, which causes them to rely on generic advice, recollection of a past consultation, or guesswork at the moment of eating. As a result, dietary self management between clinical appointments remains inconsistent, which reduces the effectiveness of prescribed treatment and contributes to avoidable long term complications.

The scale of the affected population is substantial. National surveillance data indicates that among Vietnamese adults aged 25 to 64, hypertension affects 28.3 percent and diabetes affects 7.0 percent (Vu et al., 2023). The same source reports that 56.3 percent of individuals with diabetes also have hypertension, and that the prevalence of this comorbidity increased more than eightfold between 2010 and 2021.

This comorbidity finding is significant for the design of any supporting tool. A majority of people in the target population manage more than one condition simultaneously, and the dietary constraints associated with different conditions are not identical. Guidance addressed to a single condition in isolation is therefore inadequate for most of the people who need it.

The problem is compounded by the behaviour of existing tools. Evidence from a cohort of 189,770 users of a dietary self monitoring application found that only 2.58 percent used it actively (Helander et al., 2014). The difficulty is therefore not an absence of applications but an inability to sustain their use, with the burden of manual data entry repeatedly identified as the principal cause of abandonment.

## 1.3 Project Aim

The aim of this project is to design, develop and evaluate a cross platform mobile application that combines artificial intelligence based recognition of Vietnamese dishes with proactive condition aware dietary guidance, in order to help Vietnamese adults managing diet related chronic conditions make informed daily food decisions between clinical appointments.

## 1.5 Scope and Limitations

### 1.5.1 Scope

**Table 1.2 Project scope**

| In scope | Out of scope and rationale |
|---|---|
| Mobile application for iOS and Android built with React Native | Web browser version, excluded as duplicated effort that does not reflect the point of use |
| Adults self declaring one or more of five diet related conditions | Chronic kidney disease and pregnancy, excluded because the clinical risk of incorrect guidance exceeds what can be responsibly managed without clinical supervision |
| Dish recognition by photograph and by barcode | Training a bespoke vision model, excluded as requiring a labelled dataset beyond the resources of the project |
| Condition aware guidance generated by a language model with an independent safety filter | Medical diagnosis or prescription, excluded as outside competence and regulatory permission |
| Manual logging of physical activity and body weight | Wearable device and health platform synchronisation, excluded as requiring hardware and a development build, presenting unacceptable demonstration risk |
| Community sharing of meals following a lookbook model | Private messaging and push notification while the application is closed, excluded as requiring infrastructure beyond the project scope |

### 1.5.2 Limitations

Four limitations are acknowledged at the outset and revisited in Section 8.2.2.

**Recognition accuracy.** Published accuracy for purpose built food recognition systems is approximately 86 percent (Mezgec and Koroušić Seljak, 2017), and the training datasets in common use substantially under represent non Western cuisine (Liu et al., 2025). Recognition in this application is therefore treated as a proposal requiring user confirmation rather than as a determination.

**Generative model reliability.** Output from a language model is plausible by construction but not guaranteed to be correct. This is mitigated by grounding and by independent filtering, but it cannot be eliminated.

**Self declared condition data.** The application has no access to clinical records and relies on the user's own declaration of their conditions. Guidance is only as appropriate as that declaration.

**Evaluation scale.** User Acceptance Testing with five participants establishes usability but does not establish clinical efficacy, which would require a longitudinal controlled study beyond the scope of an undergraduate project.

## 1.6 Report Structure

**Chapter 2** reviews the academic literature across three themes, namely the burden of diet related chronic conditions, the evidence for dietary management as an intervention, and the effectiveness and limitations of mobile applications as a delivery mechanism. It then critically reviews the enabling technologies and concludes by identifying the research gap this project addresses.

**Chapter 3** examines existing commercial products in the problem space, evaluating them against usability heuristics and establishing what current solutions do and do not provide.

**Chapter 4** presents the user research conducted for this project, derives personas and prioritised requirements from that research, addresses the legal, social, ethical and professional implications of the system, and documents the system architecture and interface design.

**Chapter 5** states and justifies the development methodology, records how it was adapted for single developer working, and presents the project plan, risk register and evaluation plan.

**Chapter 6** documents the implementation, covering the development environment, project structure, server and client architecture, database design and deployment.

**Chapter 7** presents the testing strategy, documented test cases, User Acceptance Testing results and requirements traceability.

**Chapter 8** evaluates the project against each stated objective, reflects critically on strengths and weaknesses, and identifies directions for future development.
